Laws For Foreign Marriage Act, 1969

Marriage Law

The Foreign Marriage Act 1969, was passed to enable the performance of marriages outside India, for registration of marriages performed outside India , and also to provide matrimonial relief in case of such marriages, and other marriages celebrated outside India. The principal provision of Act are:

(i) 'Foreign country ' means a country or place outside India and includes a ship in territorial water of such a country or place.

(ii) A marriage may be performed in a foreign country between a two persons, one of whom at least is a citizen of India by an officer of its diplomatic or consular service so designated by the Government of India provided that neither party has a spouse living, or is an idiot or lunatic, or if the man is at least 21 or the woman is at least 18, and they are not related within prohibited relationships.

(iii) A marriage officer will not, however, perform the marriage if such a marriage is prohibited by the law of the country where it is to be performed, or is inconsistent with international law or the comity of nations.

(iv) A marriage solemnized in foreign country in accordance with the formalities prescribed or permitted in such a country may be registered under the Act if the marriage officer is satisfied that it has been duly solemnized, and at least one of the parties is a citizen of India; a marriage so registered is deemed to have been solemnized under the Act.

Divorce Law

The divorce provision under the Special Marriage Act, 1954, is contained in S. 27, which is as follows:

(1) subject to the provisions of this Act and to the rules made thereunder, a petition for divorce may be presented to the district court either by the husband or the wife on the ground that the respondent.

(a) has, after the solemnization of the marriage, had voluntary sexual intercourse with any person other than his or her spouse; or

(b) has deserted the petitioner for a continuous period of not less than two years immediately preceding the presentation of the petition; or

(c) is undergoing a sentence of imprisonment for seven years or more for an offence as defined in the Indian Penal Code (45 of 1860);

(d) has since the solemnization of the marriage treated the petitioner with cruelty; or

(e) has been incurably of unsound mind, or has been suffering continuously or intermittently from mental disorder of such a kind and to such an extent that the petitioner cannot reasonably be expected to live with the respondent.

Judicial Seperation

The position under the Special Marriage Act, 1954 is the same as under the Hindu Marriage Act, 1955, and the grounds for separation and divorce are common.
