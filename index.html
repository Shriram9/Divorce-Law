Divorce in India is a matter of personal law and the law that is applicable therefore, is the law governing at that relevant time to the person belonging to a particular religion. There are different statutes governing Christians, Hindus, Parsis and Muslims. Muslims in India are largely governed both by customary law recognized by Courts and in a certain cases Muslim wives can obtain divorce under Dissolution of Muslim Marriages Act, 1939. In the State of Goa, and the Union Territories of Daman and Diu, all persons are governed by the local law applicable to marriage and divorce being the erstwhile Portugese Civil Code. Members of Tribes notified under the Constitution as 'Scheduled Tribes' are governed by their personal customary law. Every rule has an exception. Thus, while persons belonging to the particular religion can obtain matrimonial relief under the statue applicable to them, this is not an absolute rule in as much as relief under the Divorce Act, 1869 can be obtained if either party to marriage is a Christain and relief under Hindu Marriage Act, 1955 can be apparaently be obtained if a person was a Hindu and later on became a Muslim after marriage. People marriaed under Special Marriage Act, 1954 can obtain a divorce under the Act and people married under Foreign Marriage Act, 1969 or who are married outside India can obtain Divorce under Special Marriage Act, 1954.

Custody

The Guardians and Wards Act, 1890 is the enactment to be considered for dealing with the child custody. The provisions can be read in harmonious construction with the Hindu Minority and Guardianship Act, 1956 with respect to Hindus. This Act provides that, the custody of a child upon the age of five should ordinarily be with the mother. Under Muslim Law, the custody of children belongs to the mother and she cannot be deprived of her right so long as she is not found guilty of misconduct. The Indian Divorce Act, 1969 contains provisions relating to the custody of children. Christian in India can take recourse to this law.

Maintenance

Sec. 125 of the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 confers the right of maintenance on dependents including wives. The Act confers a summary remedy to be exercised by a magistrate, having territorial jurisdiction. This right is not inconsistent with the right of maintenance granted under the personal law of the parties. With respect to Christians and Parsis there appears to be no special law conferring right of maintenance on a Christen or a Parsi wife. After the enactment of Family Courts Act, 1984, the jurisdiction to grant maintenance has been transferred to the family courts. The customary Mohammedan Law confers the right of maintenance to a wife. With respect to Hindus the law has been codified in Hindu Adoption Maintenance Act, 1956.
